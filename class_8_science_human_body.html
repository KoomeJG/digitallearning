Human body

Reproduction in human beings

Reproduction is the process of giving rise to new offspring. Reproduction involves fusion of male and female sex cells. These cells are called reproductive cells.

  • A mature male produces sex cells called sperms.
  • Sperms are produced in the testes
  • A sperm has a head, a neck and a tail.
  • The head of the sperm has a nucleus.
  • The tail helps the sperm to swim in semen.

  • A mature female produces sex cells called Ova(singular -ovum).
  • The ova are produced by the ovaries
  • The release of an ovum by the ovary is known as ovulation.
  • Each ovary alternately produces an ovum once every twenty eight days.
  • The ovum has a york that covers the nucleus.
  • It has a life span of two days when not fertilised

Fertilisation

  • This is the fusion of the nucleus of the male and the female sex cells.
  • The resulting cell formed is acalled called aZygote
  • The resulting cell formed is called a Zygote.
  • Fertilisation occurs in the oviduct or fallopian tube

Implantation

After fertilisation, the zygote moves to the womb(uterus) where it attaches itself to the walls of the uterus using finger-like projections. The process by which the zygote attaches itself to the walls of the uterus is called implantation.

Foetal development

  • The zygote, after implantation, is called the embroy
  • The finger-like projections that attach the Zygote to the walls of the uterus then form the umbilical cord and Placenta
  • The embryo after eight weeks becomes a foetus and finally a baby.
  • The order of development is: Zygote-embryo-foetus-baby

Functions of the umbilical cord

The following are the functions of the umbilical code

  • It facilitates the exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide. The Oxygen moves from the mother to the embryo while carbon dioxide moves from the embryo to the placenta.
  • it allows food nutrients to pass from the mother to the foetus.
  • It also allows the transfer of watse materials from the foetus to the mother.
  • It therefore joins the foetus to the mother.

Functions of the placenta

  • Food nutrients are obtained by the foetus through the placenta.
  • It allows movement of waste material from the foetus to the mother's blood system.
  • It connects the foetus through the umbilical cord, to the mother.

Functions of the amniotic fluid

  • It keeps the foetus warm and moist
  • It protects the foetus from injuries by acting as a shock absorber.
  • It allows the foetus to move freely.
  • It lubricates the birth canal during parturition(birth)

Signs of pregnancy

The period between fertilisation and birth is known as the gestation period. During this period, the following may occur.

  • The woman cannot experience menstruation.
  • The breats become bigger, tender and sensitive.
  • The woman experiences nausea commonly referred to as morning sickness.
  • The abdomen becomes enlarged.
  • Some movements in the abdomen may be felt.
  • The skin complexion changes

The process of giving birth

  • The baby is ready to be born after 9 months or 40 weeks.
  • The baby is in position with the head facing the birth canal
  • Labour pains caused by contraction of the uterine wall muscles begin
  • The cervix dialates
  • The amniotic sac bursts and the amniotic fluid comes out.
  • The baby comes out with the head first.
  • The umbilical cord is normally tied at two points, one closer to the baby, and cut to separate the mother from the child.
  • The tying of the umbilical cord prevents excess bleeding.
  • Continued contraction of the uterine walls removes afterbirth that consists of the placenta, umbilical cord and the amnion sac.
  • The equipment used during parturition should be sterile and birth attendants should wear protective clothing.
