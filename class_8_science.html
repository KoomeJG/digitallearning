Animals

Adaptation in animals

For animals to live and survive in their environment, they need to have certain adaptations for feeding and movement.

Feeding habits of mammals

Mammals are divided into three groups according to the food they eat. These are:

  • Herbivores - Those that feed on Plants
  • Carnivores - Those that feed on flesh
  • Omnivores - Those that feed on both plants and flesh

Feeding adaptations of herbivores

  • Some feed by Grazing while others browse
  • Those that feed by grazing are called Grazers
  • Those that feed by browsing are called browsers
  • Herbivores have long rough tongues for grasping and Collecting grass
  • They have no teeth on the front part of the uper jaw. Instead, they have a horny pard thath helps in holding grass before cutting.
  • They have incisors teeth for cutting grass and premolars and molars for grinding and chewing grass.
  • They have a gap on the lower jaw between canines and premolars called the diastema.
  • The diastema is used for manipulation of the vegetation by the tongue while grazing. It also helps in grasping and rolling of the food before swallowing
  • The teeth of the herbivores grow through out since they ussually wear out during feeding
  • Examples of herbivores are Zebras,cows,goatsand sheep

Feeding adaptations of Carnivores

  • Their teeth are well-developed for various uses.
  • They have sharp, pointed canines for catching, holding and killing prey.
  • The canines are also used for tearing flesh
  • The molar and premolars are adapted for breaking bones.
  • A combination of the last premolars on the upper jaw and the first molars on the lower jaw are well-developed. They are called carnassial teeth. These are used for breaking bones and cutting big chunks of meat.
  • The teeth of carnivores are well spaced on the jaw to stop meat from getting stuck in between the teeth
  • Examples of carnivores are dogs, cats, hyenas,cheetahs and jackals

Feeding adaptations of omnivores

  • They have chisel-shaped incisors for biting and cutting food.
  • They have sharp, pointed canines for tearing flesh.
  • They have flat premolars and molars with ridges known as cusps for grinding food.
  • Examples of omnivores are human beings,gorillar and baboons

Feeding adaptations of birds

Differnt birds have beaks that vary in size and shape depending on the type of food the bird feeds on.

Grain Eaters

The beaks of grain eaters are straight, strong,thick and conical for picking grains and seeds which the birds then swallow. Examples of grain eaters are weaver birds,chicken,doves,guinea fowls and pigeons.

Filter Feeders

  • Filter feeders obtain food from water and mud.
  • The beaks of filter feeders are serrated.
  • The beaks are thick and flat.They are also strong and long.
  • The edges of the beaks have rows of connected V-shaped teeth-like structures. These are what make the beak serrated to filter food from mud. Examples of filters feeders are ducks and flamingoes.

Flesh eaters

The beaks of flesh eating birds are strong, curved, hooked and sharp for cutting and tearing flesh. Examples of flesh eaters are eagles, hawks, kites and falcons

Nector feeders

The beak of a nector feeder is slender (thin) long and slightly curved to reach the nectar in flowers. Example of a nectar feeder is a sunbird.

Adaptations of animals for movements

Animals are adapted for movement in different ways.

Adaptation to flying

  • presence of wings with light feathers for flying
  • Streamlined body to reduce air resistance during flight
  • Hollow bones which make the bird light.

Adaptation to swimming

  • Animals like frogs, have webbed feet to enable them wade in water without sinking
  • They also have streamlined bodies to reduce friction during swimming.
  • Fins as in fish help to propel and balance fish as they swim.

Adaptation to hopping and leaping

  • Strong hinf legs. Frogs, grasshoppers and kangaroos have strong hind legs for hopping and leaping.
  • They have short fore limbs to enable safe landing.

Signs of ill-health in livestock

  • Stunted growth
  • Loss of weight
  • Reduced Yields
  • Rough coat
  • Coughing
  • Blood in stool

Effects of livestock diseases

  • Lower yields
  • Reduced quality of products
  • Transimission of diseases to human beings
  • May cause death
